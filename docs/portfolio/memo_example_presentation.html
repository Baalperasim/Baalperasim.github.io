1 Intro

In 2023 there was a notable increase in wildfire activity across many regions. This memo provides an overview of where fires occurred, the data sources used, and a short spatial cluster analysis to identify hotspots that can inform monitoring and response.

2 Data

The analysis uses satellite-derived burned-area products (e.g., MODIS MCD64A1, VIIRS) aggregated to monthly grids. For each grid cell we record burn occurrence, date-of-burn, and a small set of covariates (vegetation, elevation, land cover).

Data sources: MODIS MCD64A1; Terra/Aqua burned-area composites; ancillary landcover datasets.

3 Analyzing Spatial Clusters

To detect spatial clustering we use a local indicator (e.g., Local Gi* or Getis-Ord) computed on the burned-area intensity. Areas with statistically significant high values are reported as hotspots.

3.1 Method

The steps were: (1) aggregate burned area to 0.05° grid cells; (2) compute a normalized burn intensity; (3) compute local Gi* with spatial weights based on queen contiguity.

Hotspots were identified in several regions. A simple mapping step (not included here) highlights clusters in the sample study area.

4 Conclusion

In summary, satellite-derived burned-area products provide timely estimates of fire occurrence and allow localization of high-burn clusters. Continued monitoring with these methods can support rapid situational awareness for decision-makers.
